# Blog Post Writer for B2B Systems Clarity & Operational Control

Generate high-value blog posts that resonate with CEOs, COOs, CTOs, and operations leaders at Swiss B2B companies. Content speaks to business outcomes — clarity, control, confidence — not technical jargon.

## Core Principles

Every blog post must:
1. **Identify with real pain** - Start with a specific problem readers face daily
2. **Provide actionable value** - Give concrete insights they can use immediately
3. **Build trust** - Demonstrate expertise without overselling
4. **Drive action** - Make it natural for them to want to talk to you

## Content Strategy

### Target Audience Profile

**Primary**: CEOs, COOs, CTOs, and Operations Leaders at Swiss B2B companies (50-500 employees)

**Pain points they experience:**
- Decisions based on data they can't fully trust
- Revenue delayed by broken workflows and manual handoffs
- Systems that fail when the business needs them most
- Teams doing repetitive work that software should handle
- No visibility into what's stuck or why
- Fear that AI investment will fail without reliable data first

**What they care about:**
- Clarity and control over their operations
- Data they can trust for confident decisions
- Clear ROI and measurable outcomes
- Ownership of every decision, document, and code
- Swiss quality, precision, and long-term reliability
- AI that delivers results — but only after data is solid

## Blog Post Structure

Use this proven structure for maximum engagement:

### 1. Hook (First 100 words)
Open with a specific, relatable scenario:

**Good example:**
> "It's Monday morning. Your sales team just submitted 15 quotes through Salesforce. By Thursday, only 3 made it to your ERP for fulfillment. The other 12? Stuck in someone's email, waiting for manual data entry. Your team is frustrated. Your customers are waiting. And you're wondering why the 'integration' you paid for isn't actually integrating anything."

**Why it works:**
- Concrete situation (not abstract)
- Specific numbers create credibility
- Reader sees themselves in the story
- Creates urgency without being salesy

### 2. The Real Problem (200-300 words)
Dig deeper into WHY this problem exists and WHY it matters.

Structure:
- What's really happening under the hood
- Why typical solutions fail
- What this costs (time, money, trust)
- The broader impact on operations

**Avoid:**
- Generic platitudes ("data is important")
- Obvious statements ("integrations are complex")
- Feature lists without context

### 3. A Better Way (300-400 words)
Present your approach or solution framework.

**Key elements:**
- Clear principle or methodology
- 2-3 specific examples
- Contrast with wrong approaches
- Focus on outcomes, not features

**Example structure:**
> "The problem isn't the tools. It's the architecture. Here's what works:
> 
> First, establish a single source of truth...
> 
> Second, design for failure...
> 
> Third, make it visible..."

### 4. Practical Insights (200-300 words)
Give something they can act on TODAY without hiring you.

This could be:
- A diagnostic checklist
- Key questions to ask vendors
- Red flags to watch for
- A simple framework to evaluate their situation

**Example:**
> "Before your next integration project, ask these 5 questions:
> 1. Who owns the code and documentation?
> 2. What happens when [vendor] stops supporting this?
> 3. Can your team modify this themselves?
> 4. How do we test changes without breaking production?
> 5. What's the disaster recovery plan?"

### 5. Soft CTA (50-100 words)
Make contact feel natural, not pushy. Link directly to the contact form.

**Good approach:**
> "If decisions in your company depend on data you're not sure you can trust — let's talk. I'll reply with 2-3 concrete steps to bring clarity back. No sales pitch, just straight answers.
> 
> [Contact me](#contact)"

**Link structure:**
- For homepage: `href="../#contact"`
- For service pages: `href="../#contact"`
- Button text: "Book a call" or "Get in touch" or "Contact me"

**Avoid:**
- Hard sales language
- Urgency tactics ("limited spots!")
- Feature dumping
- Comparing to competitors
- Email links or external forms

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Voice and Tone
- **Direct**: Short sentences. Clear statements.
- **Confident but humble**: "Here's what works" not "I'm the best"
- **Technical but accessible**: Use terms correctly, explain when needed
- **Swiss sensibility**: Precision, reliability, quality matter more than flash

### Language Specifics

**Do:**
- Use "I" when sharing experience: "I've seen this pattern fail repeatedly"
- Use specific numbers: "3 weeks" not "quickly"
- Use real scenarios: "When the ERP can't find the customer ID..."
- Write like you're explaining to a colleague

**Don't:**
- Use marketing speak: "game-changing", "revolutionary"
- Make vague claims: "significantly faster"
- Use unnecessary jargon when simple words work
- Write in passive voice when active is clearer

### Paragraph Structure
- **First sentence**: Make a clear point
- **Middle sentences**: Support with evidence or examples
- **Last sentence**: Transition or conclude

Keep paragraphs 3-5 sentences max for web readability.

## Post Length Guidelines

**Optimal length**: 800-1200 words
- Long enough to provide real value
- Short enough to maintain attention
- Perfect for 4-6 minute read time

## Examples of Strong Openings

### Example 1: Integration Pain
> "Last Tuesday, a Swiss manufacturer called me. They'd just paid €80,000 for a 'complete Salesforce-ERP integration.' It worked for three weeks. Then their ERP vendor pushed an update. Now nothing syncs, and their sales team is back to spreadsheets. This happens more than you'd think."

### Example 2: Automation Opportunity
> "Your operations manager spends 12 hours every week copying data from Salesforce to Excel, then uploading it to your ERP. She knows there's a better way. You know there's a better way. So why is she still doing it manually?"

### Example 3: Architecture Problem
> "Point-to-point integrations look cheap until they start multiplying. Three systems? Six connections. Five systems? Twenty connections. Ten systems? Ninety connections. Each one a potential failure point. Welcome to integration hell."

## Tone Examples

### ✅ Good Tone (Direct, Helpful, Expert)
> "Here's the truth: most integration projects fail because of architecture, not technology. The tools work fine. It's how you connect them that matters."

### ❌ Avoid (Marketing-Speak)
> "Unlock the power of seamless integration with our revolutionary approach that transforms your business operations and drives unprecedented efficiency gains."

### ✅ Good Tone (Specific, Credible)
> "In the last six months, I've reviewed 14 Salesforce-ERP integrations. Eleven had the same problem: no error handling. When something fails, it fails silently."

### ❌ Avoid (Vague, Theoretical)
> "Error handling is important for integrations and should be considered carefully during the planning phase of your project."

## Cover Image Generation

Every blog post requires a professional editorial-style cover image optimized for LinkedIn and social media sharing.

### Visual Style Guidelines

**Editorial professional aesthetic:**
- Clean, modern magazine/editorial style (like Harvard Business Review, Wired, Fast Company)
- Bold typography as visual element
- Professional photography or high-end 3D renders
- Color palette: Gold accent (#B8860B) with rich darks and clean whites
- Cinematic lighting and depth
- Tech/business editorial look
- Real-world elements (computers, offices, technology) shot professionally
- Premium quality, high polish, shareable on LinkedIn

### Image Specifications

**Technical requirements:**
- **Dimensions**: 1200x630px (optimal for Open Graph)
- **Format**: PNG or JPEG
- **File size**: < 200KB optimized
- **Aspect ratio**: 1.91:1 (Open Graph standard)

**File naming:**
- Match blog post slug: `why-salesforce-erp-integrations-break.png`
- Store in: `/blog/images/`

### Prompt Generation Process

When creating a new blog post:

1. **Analyze post content** - Identify core theme and key visual metaphor
2. **Choose template** - Select closest prompt template from examples above
3. **Customize prompt** - Adapt specific visual elements to match post content
4. **Output prompt** - Provide complete, ready-to-use AI image prompt
5. **Generate image** - User creates image with Midjourney, DALL-E, or similar
6. **Optimize** - Compress to < 200KB, save with proper filename
7. **Update HTML** - Add cover image to blog post with proper alt text

### Cover Image HTML Structure

In blog post HTML, add cover image after navigation, before article content:

```html
<div class="cover-image">
    <img src="images/your-post-slug.png" alt="[Descriptive alt text matching post topic]">
</div>
```

### Alt Text Guidelines

Write descriptive, SEO-friendly alt text:

**Good:**
- "Abstract diagram showing fragmented connection between Salesforce and ERP systems with highlighted stable connection"
- "Minimal flowchart illustrating transformation from manual to automated B2B processes"

**Avoid:**
- "Header image"
- "Blog post cover"
- Keyword stuffing
